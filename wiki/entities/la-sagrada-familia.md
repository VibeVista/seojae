La Sagrada Família is Antoni Gaudí's monumental unfinished basilica in [[Barcelona]]'s L'Eixample district — Spain's most visited monument and one of the world's most recognizable buildings. Construction began in 1882 and continues today; planned completion around 2026.

## Architecture Overview

### Nativity Façade (East)
The oldest section; Gaudí personally supervised its construction. Naturalistic sculpture — animals, plants, angels — celebrating Christ's birth. The most ornate façade.

### Passion Façade (West)
Designed by Josep Maria Subirachs after Gaudí's death; angular and deliberately austere to represent suffering. The contrast with the Nativity façade is striking.

### Glory Façade (South, under construction)
The main entrance when complete; will be the most monumental façade.

### Interior
Completed in recent decades following Gaudí's plans: branching stone columns support hyperbolic vaults; colored light floods in through stained-glass windows to create a transcendent, forest-like atmosphere.

### Towers

| Tower | Height | Status |
|-------|--------|--------|
| 8 Bell towers (Apostles) | ~100m | Complete |
| 4 Evangelists' towers | ~120m | Complete/in progress |
| Tower of the Virgin Mary | 138m | Completed 2021 |
| Tower of Jesus Christ (central) | 172.5m (planned) | Under construction |

When complete, this will be the tallest church tower in the world.

## Gaudí's Vision

Gaudí dedicated the last 16 years of his life entirely to the Sagrada Família, living in a workshop on-site. He understood it would not be completed in his lifetime and created detailed plaster models so future architects could continue his work. He described it as "the last great sanctuary of the Middle Ages."

Gaudí was killed by a tram in 1926. He is buried in the crypt of the Sagrada Família.

## Practical Tips

- **Book online without exception**: tickets sell out weeks ahead in summer, days ahead in shoulder season
- Audio guide (€14) or guided tour: essential for understanding Gaudí's symbolism and the construction story
- Tower access: lift up, walk down — not suitable for those with vertigo
- Best photography:
  - Nativity façade (east): morning light
  - Passion façade (west): afternoon light
  - Interior: when sunlight streams through stained glass (varies by season)
- Allow 1.5–2.5 hours minimum; longer if climbing towers
- Nearby: Gaudí's former house is in Park Güell — combine both on the same trip
